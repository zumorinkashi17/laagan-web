CHOCOLATE HILLS, BOHOL

The Chocolate Hills in Bohol are one of the Philippines' most iconic natural landmarks, consisting of over 1,200 limestone mounds that turn brown in the dry season, resembling chocolate drops. These geological formations spread over a 50-square-kilometer area, offer a unique and surreal landscape that is both intriguing and picturesque. The hills are covered in green grass, which turns brown during the dry season, giving them their name. Visitors can enjoy panoramic views of the hills from two main viewing decks: the Chocolate Hills Complex and Sagbayan Peak. The Chocolate Hills are a significant geological formation, believed to have formed from coral deposits millions of years ago, followed by weathering and erosion. Bohol is also home to the tarsier, one of the world's smallest primates, which can be seen at the Philippine Tarsier Sanctuary. In addition to the Chocolate Hills, Bohol offers beautiful white-sand beaches, vibrant coral reefs, and historic churches. The Loboc River Cruise is a popular activity, offering a scenic boat ride with local cuisine and cultural performances. Bohol’s friendly locals and rich culture make it a delightful destination for travelers.

What to Do:

  • - Visit the viewing decks at the Chocolate Hills Complex and Sagbayan Peak.
  • - Explore the Philippine Tarsier Sanctuary.
  • - Enjoy a Loboc River Cruise with local cuisine and cultural performances.
  • - Relax on the white-sand beaches of Panglao Island.
  • - Dive or snorkel in the vibrant coral reefs around Balicasag Island.

Travel Tips:

  • - Visit during the dry season (November to May) to see the hills in their chocolate-brown state.
  • - Rent a motorbike to explore the hills and surrounding areas.
  • - Wear comfortable shoes for walking and climbing the viewing decks.
  • - Respect wildlife, especially the sensitive tarsiers, by keeping noise levels low and avoiding flash photography.
  • - Try local delicacies like calamay and peanut kisses.
